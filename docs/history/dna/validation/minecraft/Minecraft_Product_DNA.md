## DNA Identity

DNA Statement:
Enable player-authored goals in a persistent, transformable world by linking exploration, resource acquisition, making, survival pressure, construction, and discovery into a self-renewing experience.

---

## DNA Summary

Summary:
Minecraft turns a world of manipulable resources into both the field of play and the player's expressive medium. Players explore to discover materials and places, transform resources into tools and structures, use those capabilities to reach new opportunities, and choose their own balance of survival, mastery, creativity, and shared activity. Progress is legible through expanding capability and accumulated changes to the world rather than through a single mandatory sequence.

---

## Core Experience Mechanisms

### Mechanism 1

Name:
Transformable World

Purpose:
Make the environment a persistent source of resources, problems, and player-authored outcomes.

Behavioral Effect:
Players read the world for opportunities, remove or place world elements, and leave durable changes that become evidence of effort and intent.

### Mechanism 2

Name:
Resource-to-Capability Loop

Purpose:
Convert exploration and gathering into expanded player agency.

Behavioral Effect:
Players gather accessible resources, make tools and useful items, and use the resulting capabilities to obtain previously inaccessible resources or act more effectively.

### Mechanism 3

Name:
Survival Pressure

Purpose:
Create immediate priorities and meaningful tradeoffs without prescribing one solution.

Behavioral Effect:
Threats, limited safety, health, and hunger encourage players to seek shelter, food, equipment, and preparation while preserving multiple viable responses.

### Mechanism 4

Name:
Player-Authored Construction

Purpose:
Turn acquired resources and spatial understanding into functional or expressive creations.

Behavioral Effect:
Players build shelters, infrastructure, landmarks, machines, or decorative works whose value is defined by player goals and continued use.

### Mechanism 5

Name:
Layered Discovery

Purpose:
Sustain curiosity by placing distinct resources, hazards, structures, creatures, and destinations across the world.

Behavioral Effect:
Each discovery can reveal a new goal, capability requirement, route, collection target, or reason to prepare for another expedition.

### Mechanism 6

Name:
Emergent System Interaction

Purpose:
Let simple world rules combine into outcomes beyond individually authored tasks.

Behavioral Effect:
Players experiment with materials, creatures, environmental rules, and signal-based mechanisms to create farms, transport, traps, machines, and other self-defined systems.

### Mechanism 7

Name:
Shared World Agency

Purpose:
Allow individual activity to acquire social meaning in a common persistent space.

Behavioral Effect:
Players cooperate, divide work, exchange resources, display creations, establish shared norms, or pursue parallel goals whose effects remain visible to others.

## Mechanism Interaction Model

Interaction Description:
Exploration exposes resources, places, and risks. Gathering converts discovered resources into available inputs; making converts those inputs into capabilities; capabilities increase survival, construction, automation, and access to further discoveries. Construction persists as utility, expression, or preparation, while survival pressure periodically redirects attention toward immediate needs. The changed world then becomes the starting state for the next self-authored goal. In shared worlds, each participant's changes can become resources, constraints, landmarks, or invitations for others.

## User Motivation Model

### Primary Motivation

* Exercise autonomy by choosing what to pursue and how to pursue it.
* Convert imagination and effort into persistent, inspectable changes.
* Discover unfamiliar places, resources, behaviors, and possibilities.

### Secondary Motivation

* Increase competence through preparation, experimentation, and mastery.
* Accumulate resources, capabilities, territory, collections, and completed creations.
* Survive hazards and recover from setbacks.
* Cooperate, exchange, perform, or build a shared history with other players.

---

## Experience Principles

### Principle 1

The world must support many valid goals and must not require a single prescribed play sequence.

### Principle 2

Actions should produce durable consequences that can become inputs to later actions.

### Principle 3

Progress should expand capability and possibility rather than only increase abstract rank.

### Principle 4

Constraints and hazards should generate priorities while leaving room for player-devised solutions.

### Principle 5

Discovery should repeatedly connect unfamiliar content to new goals, preparation, or experimentation.

### Principle 6

Simple reusable rules should combine into functional and expressive outcomes not limited to predefined tasks.

### Principle 7

Failure should create recoverable loss, adaptation, or a new objective rather than terminate the broader experience.

## Applicable Scenarios

* Open-ended products centered on player or user agency.
* Persistent environments that participants can materially change.
* Experiences where gathering, making, and expanded capability can form a repeatable loop.
* Creative systems that benefit from functional and expressive construction.
* Exploration experiences sustained by distributed resources, risks, and discoveries.
* Shared spaces where participant-created changes can influence later activity.

---

## Non-Applicable Scenarios

* Strictly linear experiences whose value depends on one authored sequence.
* Products where user-created state cannot persist or influence later activity.
* Experiences requiring identical goals, methods, and outcomes for every participant.
* Short single-purpose workflows that do not benefit from exploration, accumulation, or systemic experimentation.

---

## Combination Guidance

### Compatible DNA Characteristics

* Preserves player choice among multiple meaningful goals.
* Adds activities that feed resources, capabilities, discoveries, creations, or shared-world consequences back into the core loop.
* Supports persistent outcomes and reusable systemic interactions.

### Potential Conflict Characteristics

* Imposes a dominant mandatory sequence that makes self-authored goals irrelevant.
* Replaces world-based consequences with progression that exists only as abstract status.
* Makes creation disposable, nonfunctional, or disconnected from future activity.

## Risks

### Known Risks

* Broad freedom can leave players without a clear near-term goal.
* Resource loss can turn meaningful risk into disproportionate repetition.
* Expanding possibility can overwhelm players before they understand available actions.
* Repetitive gathering can dominate if new capabilities do not materially change play.
* Persistent shared changes can create conflict when ownership and expectations differ.
* Powerful systemic interactions can invalidate survival pressure or other activities if their outcomes are not balanced against effort and discovery.

## References

### Observed Product Behaviors

* In Survival mode, players gather resources, make tools, manage health and hunger, respond to hostile creatures, and build shelter while retaining discretion over their longer-term goals.
* Blocks can be removed, collected, transformed, and placed, allowing player actions to persist as structures, routes, farms, machines, and environmental changes.
* Material access and tool capability form nested loops: early resources enable tools that make additional resources and activities reachable.
* Distinct environments, underground areas, generated structures, creatures, and dimensions distribute discoveries and hazards across space.
* Construction can be protective, productive, navigational, experimental, decorative, or social without requiring separate goal systems.
* Redstone and other interacting world rules let players create signal-driven mechanisms and automated behaviors.
* Multiplayer worlds allow participants to cooperate, exchange resources, divide work, and encounter one another's persistent changes.
